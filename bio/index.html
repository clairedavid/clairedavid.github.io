Bio

How it started

At around 15 I had a lot of questions for my science teachers. So many questions. It started to become uncomfortable and I was thinking of stopping asking questions once and for all. But in the following summer I was sent to a science camp where I met people my age having fun with mathematical riddles until 3:00 AM. Discomfort turned into an acknowledged passion. These new friends had the book “The Universe in a Nutshell”, by Stephen Hawking. When I saw a picture of one of the big particle detectors at CERN (at that time under construction), I became a fan. By then my life plan was “I want to be near particle detectors”.

After high school, I was reluctant to go the elitist routes of the French educational system. Influenced by my parents, I enrolled in a Master’s of Engineering at INSA, the National Institute of Applied Sciences in Toulouse. But I dreamt of CERN, not applied sciences. Luckily I took particle physics classes during an exchange semester at Humboldt-Universität zu Berlin in Germany, while working in parallel on detector electronics at the laboratory DESY in Zeuthen. An experimental physicist had a fancy plot on his screen and I asked him how one gains knowledge from the data (and makes fancy plots). He was busy and replied: “You will have to do a Ph.D. for that.”

One year and 8000 km later, I completed my last engineering internship at TRIUMF in Vancouver, Canada and received a Ph.D. offer with the University of Victoria. I got the answer to my question (while getting paid), I made fancy plots and… I developed thousands of new questions! I came across a multitude of new topics, one of them being machine learning, which I found absolutely fascinating.

During this adventure into knowledge-making, I also discovered another passion: teaching. I try my best to captivate, share the joys of science as well as help students catch their dreams. I encourage them to ask questions, many questions, as it could be the start of someone’s journey…


A special year in Africa

In the middle of my tenure track as assistant professor, I decided to take a leave for an entire year. Not a sabbatical but a leave of absence. Why? I was frustrated to not have the time to learn, especially the AI tools I was using in my own research. I was also perturbed by climate change and biodiversity collapse. What’s the point of doing high energy physics on a burning and dying planet?

From September 2022, I trained myself in AI - diving into the math of machine learning - to build the dream course I have always wanted to teach. I left for South Africa in November and attended the African School of Physics ASP2022 in Gqeberha, where I received several invitations.

Overall, I spent six months in the continent and gave colloquia in eight African countries. I taught my course at AIMS Senegal, South Africa and Ghana, visited the other AIMS centres of Cameroon and Rwanda. I took part of the Science and Technology Week festival in Congo DRC where I introduced the CERN Large Hadron Collider to 5,000 Congolese high school students.

I was sponsored by the Arthur B McDonald Canadian Astroparticle Physics Research Institute, who named me their ambassador. You can read more about my special year on their news page in this article.

Last but not least: I found a new job. While at AIMS South Africa, I was offered the opportunity to coordinate a new master’s course, funded by Google DeepMind and delivered at AIMS South Africa: AI For Science. The initial focus areas of the program will be AI and machine learning in cosmology, epidemiology and ecology.

It is our hope that many more students in Africa can be involved in pioneering research
and contribute to the scientific discoveries that are yet to come.
