Current research

The ATLAS detector at the CERN Large Hadron Collider

Lying in an underground tunnel of 27 km circumference under the French-Swiss border near Geneva, the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) accelerates particles close to the speed of light and lets them smash together at the centre of big detectors. The task for experimental particle physicists is to reconstruct the trajectories and identify the particles produced during these collisions, to then measure their properties for ultimately inferring on the presence of new processes in subatomic physics.

The ATLAS detector under construction (left) and later on (right), both pictures contain humans for scale. Credit: ATLAS @ CERN

One of the LHC’s detectors is ATLAS (A Toroidal LHC ApparatuS). It is an arrangement of concentric sub-detectors - each recording specific information from the particles produced in a collision - reaching the size of a five story-building. This short video from the great movie Particle Fever gives an idea of what it feels like seeing ATLAS for the first time.

I started my Ph.D. just before the big LHC’s success in 2012: the discovery of the Higgs boson, a long-sought particle responsible for giving massive particles their observed masses (the understanding of mass at the subatomic). I was relieved as my Ph.D. analysis required the presence of the Higgs boson! Moving on as a postdoc, I contributed to the observation of a rare process, also involving the Higgs boson: its production in association with a top quark pair (quarks are constituents of protons and neutrons). I also had fun playing with an infrared camera to characterise the thermal performance of the next generation of particle detectors; this was part of the ATLAS upgrade program, preparing for an enhanced collision phase called the High-Luminosity LHC Project.

Current interests in ATLAS:
Anomaly detection for discovering New Physics, real-time Machine Learning using dedicated hardware (FPGA) for the trigger system.

Past Projects

DUNE

Between 2019 and 2022, I helped launch the Canadian group on the Deep Underground Neutrino Experiment: DUNE. It is a future leading-edge detector complex, hosted at the Long-Baseline Neutrino Facility (LBNF) at Fermilab near Chicago and at the Sanford Underground Research Facility (SURF) in the former Homestake Mine in Lead, South Dakota.

DUNE is dedicated to study one mysterious elementary particle: the neutrino. They are the most abundant matter particle in the universe yet very difficult to detect. DUNE will measure how neutrinos change over time, a phenomenon known as oscillations. DUNE will also be able to observe neutrinos from a core-collapse supernova in the Milky Way, allowing us to peer inside a newly-formed neutron star and potentially witness the birth of a black hole.

My research was mainly within the DUNE Computing Consortium. I organized the training to help newcomers start with the software suite. I also led the first R&D on an interactive analysis facility for DUNE using columnar computing.

IceCube

My first particle physics experience started at DESY in Zeuthen, near Berlin, Germany. I was still an engineer-padawan, working on the analog front-end electronics of the detector modules for the IceCube Neutrino Observatory, located in Antarctica.
